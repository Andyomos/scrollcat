Here's the dirty secret of almost every "on-chain" game: the house rolls the dice.

The randomness that decides your crit, your dodge, your loot — it's generated on a server you can't see, by people who profit when you lose. You're asked to trust that they didn't tip the scale. In crypto, of all places, "trust me" is the punchline.

ScrollCat does the opposite. We didn't try to earn your trust. We built a system where trust isn't required.

Every fight in the Arena is provably fair — seeded by randomness baked into the blockchain itself, in a way that nobody, including us, can predict or rig. Here's how it actually works, and why it's the whole reason ScrollCat lives on Supra.

Whoever Controls the Dice Controls the Game

Randomness sounds like a small technical detail. It isn't. It's the entire integrity of a game.

If the house generates the random number, the house can choose it. Maybe it never does — but you have no way to know, and "no way to know" is exactly the problem. Every reward, every rare outcome, every PvP win becomes a question mark with a server behind it.

Most projects hand-wave this. They say "fair" and hope you don't ask the next question. We think the next question is the only one that matters: prove it.

SECTION 3 — THE ANSWER: dVRF [mark first line as heading with H]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Randomness With a Receipt

ScrollCat's combat draws its randomness from Supra dVRF — a decentralized Verifiable Random Function built into the chain. Two words carry the weight: decentralized and verifiable.

Decentralized means no single party produces the number — not another player, not the dev, not a hidden server. Verifiable means every random value comes with a cryptographic proof that anyone can check. The number is provably unpredictable before the roll and provably untampered after it.

So when your cat lands a crit, that wasn't us being generous. It was math you could audit. Same when you lose — it wasn't us punishing you. Nobody had a thumb on the scale, because the scale won't allow a thumb.

A Match Is Just Math You Can Replay

The randomness is half of it. The combat engine is the other half — and it's deterministic and open.

A ScrollCat match is fully described by four things: your cat, your opponent, the dVRF seed, and the sequence of moves both players chose. Feed those back in and the fight resolves the exact same way, every time. Anyone can replay any match and confirm the winner. No black box. No "the server says you lost."

That's what fairness actually looks like when you build it instead of claiming it: a fight that's a public, repeatable calculation — not a story the house tells you afterward.
